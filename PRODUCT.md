# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Lena and a management team of roughly 5–15 people at an Israeli FM (Facility Management) company. Mixed technical levels — some are comfortable reading raw financial data, others want a quick "is this OK or not?" signal. They open this tool to answer a specific question: which projects are profitable, which are bleeding, and what's the cash position. They're not browsing; they're checking.

Primary language: Hebrew. Interface is RTL throughout.

## Product Purpose

Logfi gives the management team real-time per-project visibility into P&L, cash flow, and risk — replacing a world where profitability was invisible until end-of-quarter Excel consolidation. Success means a manager can open the app and know in under 30 seconds whether any project needs attention.

Four pillars, all equally important:
1. Per-project P&L visibility — which sites make or lose money, each month.
2. Cash flow forecasting — cumulative and monthly net liquidity.
3. Risk alerts / early warning — surface margin problems before they become crises.
4. AI CFO layer — natural-language chat over the data, risk scoring, what-if simulations.

Internal tool for one company only. Not SaaS, not multi-tenant.

## Brand Personality

Sharp · Confident · Direct

The interface should feel like a Bloomberg terminal that doesn't hate you — opinionated, precise, nothing decorative. Numbers are the product; chrome is infrastructure. When a manager opens a view, the most important number is immediately obvious without scanning.

Reference: Stripe Dashboard — light, data-forward, numbers as hero, UI disappears.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS cream**: beige/off-white backgrounds, rounded-everything, friendly gradients, the homogeneous B2B look. The app should have a stronger point of view.
- **Excel / government**: dense grey tables, no visual hierarchy, legacy enterprise feel. This is exactly what Logfi replaces.
- **Healthcare teal**: over-reliance on teal as a calming brand color — too clinical, too generic. Teal exists in the palette as an accent, not as a personality.
- **Crypto / Web3 dark**: neon on black, aggressive gradients, VC-deck energy. Nothing here.

## Design Principles

1. **Numbers win the surface.** Every layout decision asks: does this help the number be read faster? Decoration that doesn't serve data is removed, not reduced.
2. **Hierarchy through scale, not ornament.** Contrast comes from typographic weight and size. Not borders, gradients, or icon forests.
3. **Answer the question before it's asked.** Every financial figure should carry its own verdict — color, tone, or pill — so the reader doesn't need a second step to interpret it.
4. **Speed over comfort.** Managers check this quickly. Critical data gets the most surface area; secondary detail is a scroll or a click away. Above-the-fold real estate is reserved for the most actionable number.
5. **Earn every pixel.** No padding that isn't doing work. No card that isn't the best container for its content. Empty space is intentional; filler space is a bug.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum throughout. Body text and key data must meet 4.5:1 contrast ratio. Decorative text (disabled states, whisper labels) may use the lower 3:1 threshold but never for data that needs to be read. RTL is structural, not a workaround — chart labels, form flows, and table layouts all respect right-to-left reading order. No reliance on color alone for meaning; always pair color with shape, icon, or label.
